From familiar number rules to the first foothills of category theory

This page starts with the kind of questions a strong high-school student might ask. What sorts of numbers are there? What rules do they obey? Why do maps between structures matter? It then gently turns toward the undergraduate language of monoids, groups, morphisms, and functors.

The objects matter, but the rules they obey and the maps that preserve those rules matter just as much.

A first idea

The same underlying set can be viewed in different ways depending on which operations and mappings we care about.

1 · Number types

What changes as our number systems grow?

Naturals, \(\mathbb{N}\), are the counting numbers. They are good for counting collections and building patterns.

Typical question: can we always subtract? Not inside \(\mathbb{N}\).

Integers, \(\mathbb{Z}\), add negative numbers and zero. Now subtraction becomes far more natural.

We have extended the system so that more equations can be solved.

Rationals, \(\mathbb{Q}\), are numbers of the form \(p/q\) with \(q\neq 0\). Fractions let division work much more often.

These are still built from integer data, but the rules become more flexible.

Reals, \(\mathbb{R}\), include limits, decimal expansions, roots like \(\sqrt{2}\), and values defined by infinite processes.

This is where infinite series and functions like \(\zeta(s)\) start to matter.

3 · Infinite series

Even and odd: a surprising asymmetry

The Riemann zeta function is defined by

\[\zeta(s)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^s}.\]

At even positive integers, Euler found exact closed forms. The simplest is the Basel result:

\[\zeta(2)=\frac{\pi^2}{6}.\]

Every even value \(\zeta(2n)\) turns out to be a rational multiple of \(\pi^{2n}\). The Bernoulli numbers supply the rational part. \(\pi\) supplies the transcendental part. We will see this bridge in section 10.

At odd positive integers the situation is quite different. Apéry proved in 1978 that \(\zeta(3)\) is irrational, but no closed form in terms of \(\pi\) is known. The odd zeta-values remain largely mysterious.

An infinite sum of rational terms can land on a rational multiple of \(\pi^{2n}\) or on something we barely understand, depending on whether the exponent is even or odd.

5 · Structures

From school algebra to monoids and groups

At school, we usually learn rules one topic at a time. Undergraduate algebra asks: which collections of objects satisfy which rules?

Semigroup

An operation that is associative.

Combining any three objects gives the same result no matter how we bracket.

Monoid

A semigroup with an identity.

Integers under multiplication: 1 leaves values unchanged.

Group

A monoid where every element has an inverse.

Integers under addition: every \(a\) has inverse \(-a\).

Abelian group

A group where the operation also commutes.

Example: \((\mathbb{Z},+)\) and \((\mathbb{R},+)\).

Ring

Two operations tied by distributivity.

Addition forms an abelian group; multiplication is associative.

Field

A ring where non-zero elements have multiplicative inverses.

Example: \(\mathbb{Q},\;\mathbb{R},\;\mathbb{C}\).

7 · Concrete examples

The same set, different structures

The structure depends on which operation we attach. Four cases that make the distinctions tangible.

Positive integers under ×
Associative, identity 1, but no inverses: there is no positive integer \(n\) with \(3n=1\). A monoid.
Rotations of an equilateral triangle
Three rotations (0°, 120°, 240°). Associative, identity 0°, every rotation undone. A group (cyclic, order 3, abelian).
2×2 real matrices under ×
Associative with identity \(I\), but \(AB\neq BA\) in general and not every matrix is invertible. A non-commutative monoid.
Integers mod 5 with + and ×
\(\{0,1,2,3,4\}\) with arithmetic mod 5. Every non-zero element has a multiplicative inverse (e.g. \(2\times 3\equiv 1\)). A field, \(\mathbb{F}_5\).

9 · Composition

Why morphisms matter

A morphism is a map that respects structure. For groups, a homomorphism \(f\) satisfies

\[f(a\star b)=f(a)\diamond f(b).\]

10 · The π-bridge

\(\pi\) as the bridge between zeta-values and Bernoulli numbers

Every even zeta-value factorises into three pieces: a sign, a rational factor built from the Bernoulli number \(B_{2n}\), and a power of \(\pi\). Isolating \(\pi\) as the bridge term:

\[ \zeta(2n)\;=\; \underbrace{(-1)^{n+1}\,\frac{B_{2n}}{2\,(2n)!}}_{\text{rational}}\;\times\; \underbrace{(2\pi)^{2n}}_{\color{#ffd166}{\pi\text{-bridge}}} \]

The rational part is arithmetic. The \(\pi^{2n}\) part is geometric and transcendental. The formula locks these two worlds together at every even integer.

11 · Final reflection

What begins as number rules turns into a study of relations

The journey starts with familiar algebraic laws, moves through infinite series and special numbers, and arrives at a broader point: mathematics gains power when it studies structure and the mappings that preserve it. School mathematics often asks us to compute. Higher mathematics asks what sort of thing we are computing in, and what kinds of maps make sense there.

The \(\pi\)-bridge is a case in point. Two apparently separate families of numbers turn out to be locked together by a single transcendental constant. The instinct to look for such correspondences between whole systems, rather than isolated formulas, is exactly the instinct that category theory formalises.
