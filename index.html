From familiar number rules to the first foothills of category theory

This page starts with the kind of questions a strong high-school student might ask. What sorts of numbers are there? What rules do they obey? Why do maps between structures matter? It then gently turns toward the undergraduate language of monoids, groups, morphisms, and functors.

The objects matter, but the rules they obey and the maps that preserve those rules matter just as much.

A first idea

The same underlying set can be viewed in different ways depending on which operations and mappings we care about.

1 · Number types

What changes as our number systems grow?

Naturals, \(\mathbb{N}\), are the counting numbers. They are good for counting collections and building patterns.

Typical question: can we always subtract? Not inside \(\mathbb{N}\).

Integers, \(\mathbb{Z}\), add negative numbers and zero. Now subtraction becomes far more natural.

We have extended the system so that more equations can be solved.

Rationals, \(\mathbb{Q}\), are numbers of the form \(p/q\) with \(q\neq 0\). Fractions let division work much more often.

These are still built from integer data, but the rules become more flexible.

Reals, \(\mathbb{R}\), include limits, decimal expansions, roots like \(\sqrt{2}\), and values defined by infinite processes.

This is where infinite series and functions like \(\zeta(s)\) start to matter.

3 · Infinite series

Irrationals can arise from infinite sums

Some important real numbers are reached through infinite series. A famous example is the Riemann zeta function

\[ \zeta(s)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^s}. \]

At \(s=2\), Euler showed that

\[ \zeta(2)=\frac{\pi^2}{6}. \]

This is striking: an infinite rational-looking sum is tied to \(\pi\), a geometric irrational.

The big shift is philosophical as well as technical: a number may be understood not only as a decimal or a point on a line, but as the value of an infinite process.

5 · Structures

From school algebra to monoids and groups

At school, we usually learn rules one topic at a time. Undergraduate algebra asks a different question: which collections of objects satisfy which rules?

Semigroup

An operation that is associative.

A set where combining any three objects gives the same result no matter how we bracket them.

Monoid

A semigroup with an identity element.

Example: integers under multiplication, where 1 leaves values unchanged.

Group

A monoid in which every element has an inverse.

Example: integers under addition; every number \(a\) has inverse \(-a\).

Abelian group

A group where the operation also commutes.

Example: \((\mathbb{Z},+)\) and \((\mathbb{R},+)\).

Ring

A set with both addition and multiplication, tied together by distributivity.

Here addition usually forms an abelian group.

Field

A ring in which non-zero elements also have multiplicative inverses.

Example: \(\mathbb{Q},\mathbb{R},\mathbb{C}\).

7 · Reflection

Why name these structures at all?

Because once we name a pattern, we can reuse theorems across many settings. The point is economy of thought.

High-school reflection

The same number system can feel different depending on which operation we care about. Integers are not a group under multiplication, but they are under addition. So the "thing" is not just the set: it is the set together with a chosen operation.

Undergraduate tee-up

This shift prepares the way for abstract algebra. We stop asking only "what is this number?" and start asking "what kind of algebraic object is this, and which maps preserve its structure?"

9 · Composition

Why morphisms matter

A morphism is not just any map. It is a map that respects the structure we care about. For groups, that usually means a homomorphism.

A group homomorphism \(f\) satisfies

\[ f(a\star b)=f(a)\diamond f(b). \]

So the operation in the source is mirrored by the operation in the target.

10 · A bridge between two families

From zeta-values to Bernoulli numbers

One classical bridge is

\[ \zeta(2n)=(-1)^{n+1}\frac{B_{2n}(2\pi)^{2n}}{2(2n)!}. \]

This relation links even zeta-values to Bernoulli numbers. It is not a full category-theoretic development on its own, but it is a beautiful example of a rule carrying information from one family of mathematical objects to another.

⇔
Bridge term

Careful language

At this stage, it is safest to say there is a structured relationship or bridge between the two families. Calling something a functor requires a precise specification of categories, objects, and morphisms.

Why still mention functors?

Because the instinct is right: modern mathematics often tries to understand not isolated formulas, but the ways whole systems correspond.

11 · Final reflection

What begins as number rules turns into a study of relations

The journey starts with familiar algebraic laws, moves through infinite series and special numbers, and arrives at a broader point: mathematics gains power when it studies structure and the mappings that preserve it. School mathematics often asks us to compute; higher mathematics asks us what sort of thing we are computing in, and what kinds of maps make sense there.
